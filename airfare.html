The Problem

Airline ticket pricing is one of the most complex revenue management challenges in commercial aviation. Fares fluctuate based on route, carrier, departure timing, booking window, seat class, and demand dynamics — often within the same hour. Understanding the relative weight of each driver is critical for pricing strategy, competitive benchmarking, and yield optimisation.

This project builds a predictive model for airline ticket fares, treating the problem as a structured regression task and evaluating six distinct modelling approaches to identify the most reliable solution.

Data & Feature Engineering

Business variables — route, airline, departure timing, and booking characteristics — were translated into structured analytical features. Key engineering steps included encoding categorical variables (carrier, origin/destination), extracting time-based features (day of week, time-to-departure, season), and normalising continuous inputs for model comparability.

Models evaluated across the six approaches included:

  • Linear regression (baseline)
  • Ridge and Lasso regularised regression
  • Decision tree regressor
  • Random forest ensemble
  • Gradient boosting (XGBoost)

Gradient boosting delivered the highest accuracy at 98.4%, with departure timing and booking window emerging as the most influential fare drivers — outweighing route distance in predictive importance.

Decision-Support Output

Findings were presented through an interactive decision-support tool designed for non-technical stakeholders — translating model outputs into plain-language pricing implications. This reflects a core principle of commercial analytics: precision in the model is wasted if the insight doesn't reach the decision-maker in an actionable form.

Commercial Implications

For a revenue management or commercial performance team, this model surfaces two practical applications: a competitive fare benchmarking tool (comparing predicted fares against actual market pricing) and a yield sensitivity analyser (quantifying how fare changes in response to timing and demand shifts). Both are directly relevant to dynamic pricing and schedule planning functions within an airline commercial team.
